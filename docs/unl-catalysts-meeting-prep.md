# Catalysts Pilot Meeting — Mallory

## Context

- Mallory runs Catalysts program at UNL Center for Entrepreneurship
- She's already seen mkrs earlier this year, said the platform would be "super helpful" for Catalysts
- First mkrs event had 55 students from every college — she knows this
- She offered to explore event sponsorship in the future
- Lindsay is the contact for Accelerator (separate conversation)
- This meeting is about the Catalysts pilot specifically

## Goal

Walk out with:
1. A plan to get mkrs in front of Catalysts students (when, how)
2. Clarity on what success looks like for her
3. If the door opens — redirect the event sponsorship budget toward the pilot

## Opening — Ask, Don't Pitch

Start by understanding her world. Don't jump into mkrs.

- "I'd love to understand more about Catalysts before we figure out how mkrs fits in."
- How many students are in the program right now?
- What's the structure — cohort-based? Semester-long? Rolling?
- What are you trying to get students to do / get out of the program?
- Where do you feel like students are getting stuck or where could the experience be better?
- How do students in the program currently connect with each other outside of scheduled programming?

**Listen for pain points.** She'll probably mention some version of:
- Students don't collaborate enough outside of class
- Hard to measure program impact
- Connections don't last beyond the program
- Students stay in their own circles even within the program

Whatever she says — that's what mkrs addresses. Don't manufacture the fit. Let her tell you where it is.

## Middle — Connect Her Needs to What You've Built

Keep it conversational. Only bring up what's relevant to what she just said.

**If she mentions students not connecting enough:**
- mkrs matches students based on what they're working on and what they need — not just shared interests, but complementary skills
- "A business student looking for a developer gets matched with a CS student looking for a startup to join"
- The feed is where students post what they need — "looking for a designer," "who's taken this class" — and other students respond or get tagged

**If she mentions measuring impact:**
- We track every connection, every conversation started, where connections came from
- "At the end of the pilot I can show you exactly how many connections Catalysts students made, across how many disciplines, how many turned into real conversations"

**If she mentions engagement dropping off:**
- Weekly match notifications bring students back — "we found someone you should know"
- The profile compounds — vouches, projects, connections — so there's a reason to stay active
- Students who meet at events stay connected through the platform

**The 55-person event:**
- Mention it naturally as proof this works — "At our first event we had 55 students from every college. The platform keeps those connections alive after the event ends."

## Addressing Stickiness (She's Asked About This Before)

She's already raised this question — lean into it. Don't wait for her to ask again. Bring it up yourself to show you've been thinking about it.

> "You asked before about how we make this sticky. Here's what we've built around that."

**The profile compounds — that's the switching cost.**
- Every vouch, every project, every connection lives on your mkrs profile
- The longer you use it, the more valuable your profile becomes
- Leaving means losing your reputation — that's what keeps people
- "It's like a portfolio that builds itself as you use the app"

**Students don't need to remember to open the app — the app comes to them.**
- Weekly match notifications: "We found someone you should know" — 2-3 per week, personalized with a reason
- Opportunity alerts: when someone posts looking for a skill you have, you get notified
- Weekly prompt: "What are you working on this week?" — low-effort way to stay visible
- Post-connection nudge: "You connected with Sarah 3 days ago — say hey" with a suggested conversation starter

**Missing something has a real cost.**
- Someone posted "looking for a designer for my startup" and you didn't see it — that's an opportunity you missed
- It's not doomscrolling FOMO. It's "someone needed exactly what I offer and I wasn't there"

**It's a weekly tool, not a daily addiction.**
- Students open it 2-3 times a week, spend 3-5 minutes, and close it
- That's enough to build real connections over a semester
- "We're not trying to compete with Instagram for screen time. We're trying to make sure the right people find each other."

If she pushes further on retention specifics, you can mention:
- The first event showed 55 students engaged enough to show up in person — the platform keeps that energy going between events
- Students who make even one real connection in the first week are significantly more likely to stay active
- The data from the pilot will tell us exactly what's working and what's not — that's part of what we'd report back

## The Pilot Proposal

Keep it simple and low-commitment.

- Roll out mkrs to the Catalysts cohort
- I come in for 10-15 minutes at a Catalysts meeting to onboard them (or async if easier)
- Students fill out profiles, get matched with each other and other students on the platform
- 4-6 week pilot
- At the end, I share data: connections made, conversations started, cross-discipline interactions, student feedback
- "I'll handle everything. You just get me in front of the students."

**Ask her:**
- "What would make this a success in your eyes? What would you want to see at the end?"
- Write down whatever she says. That's your success metric.

## The Money Conversation (If It Feels Right)

Don't lead with this. Only bring it up toward the end, after the pilot is agreed on.

- "You'd mentioned exploring sponsorship for events. What we've found is the events and the platform work together — events drive students to the platform, the platform keeps them connected after."
- "Instead of sponsoring individual events, would it make sense to put that support behind the pilot itself? We'd handle everything — onboarding, technology, data reporting — and you'd get a full report at the end."
- Frame it as redirecting existing budget, not a new expense
- $1,500-3,000 for a semester pilot is reasonable
- Deliverables: onboarding session, monthly check-ins, end-of-pilot data report

**If she hesitates or needs approvals:**
- "Totally fine — we can start the pilot now at no cost and formalize it based on results. No risk on your end and we can move fast."
- Don't let money slow down getting users on the platform

## What NOT to Do

- Don't demo every feature — only show what's relevant to what she said
- Don't mention the business model, university data revenue, employer play, or monetization
- Don't oversell — "let's try it and see" not "this will transform your program"
- Don't make it about mkrs — make it about her students
- If she asks how you make money: "We're focused on building something students actually use first. The business side comes later."

## After the Meeting

Send a follow-up email within 2 hours. Short, specific, actionable:

- Recap what you agreed on
- Confirm the onboarding date/format
- Attach or describe what you'll deliver (the data report at end of pilot)
- Thank her

## Questions to Have Ready If She Asks

**"What makes this different from LinkedIn / GroupMe / etc?"**
- LinkedIn is for professionals performing for recruiters. mkrs is for students finding each other right now — who on campus can help with X, who should I know.
- GroupMe is for groups that already exist. mkrs connects people who don't know each other yet.

**"How do you keep students engaged?"**
- Weekly match notifications — "we found someone you should know"
- Opportunity alerts when someone posts something matching your skills
- Weekly prompt — "what are you working on this week?"
- The profile itself compounds — vouches, projects, connections — gives people a reason to maintain it

**"What data would we see?"**
- Connections made (total, by discipline, cross-major)
- Conversations started from those connections
- Where connections came from (match, feed post, event, search)
- Student feedback
- Whatever metrics matter most to you — happy to customize

**"Is this just for entrepreneur types?"**
- No — any student doing something beyond just going to class. Film students, designers, business students, anyone with a skill or a need. The first event had students from every college.
